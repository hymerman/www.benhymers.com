Games

Cities in the Sky

Cities in the Sky is a game about building on a floating island whilst keeping it balanced, and keeping the little people living on it alive.

I've been developing it on and off since I was at university; it was originally intended as a portfolio piece but now serves more as a sort of test-bed for my development ideas. It is still progressing as a game of course, just very slowly due to my day job and due to me testing out new things on it!

It's written in C++, using several middleware libraries (OGRE, Bullet, irrKlang, OIS). The 'engine' part of it is a separate library, 'bengine' (I know), which is also in use by Banditos. It has a very flexible game object system based around components, which I have spent a lot of time designing and implementing.

I'm the only programmer on CITS, and I'm also doing the modelling, texturing, rigging, animation, 2D art, sound and music. I figure it's an excellent way to get a deep understanding of the whole of the game development process!

Banditos vs. Federales

A local multiplayer stealth game, pitting a team of banditos trying to steal everything they can, against a team of federales trying to stop them.

Banditos vs. Federales is a game I am developing with some colleagues from Rare, outside of work, for a bit of fun. We're massive fans of local multiplayer games, especially cooperative games with competitive undertones. Banditos was born out of the need for a perfect lunchtime game to play at work!

Like CITS, Banditos is programmed in C++ using several middleware libraries, tied together using bengine. The flexibility of bengine's object system means programming gameplay is mostly a case of writing script-like components, and composing objects from those.

Kinect Sports

Kinect Sports is the current #1 sports game for the Xbox Kinect sensor, which at the time of writing has sold around 2.72 million copies according to VGChartz.

I worked on Kinect Sports as a gameplay engineer, working solely on the table tennis, one of the six sports included. I was the only programmer working on the sport full-time, but there were two programmers including myself working on it regularly.

On table tennis, I wrote the Kinect gestures, game logic, AI, animation, physics, and plenty of other assorted bits that needed doing. It was of course a massive challenge since programming for Kinect is a genuinely new area of technology, but the reviews and sales indicate that I did well :).

It feels good knowing that I am literally one of the most experienced Kinect gesture programmers in the world, having been involved in Kinect Sports and various unreleased prototypes from a very early stage.
